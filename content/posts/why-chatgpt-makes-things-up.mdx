You asked ChatGPT for a stat, dropped it into a deck, and someone in the meeting asked where it came from. You checked. The number was wrong. Not rounded-off wrong. Invented. The source it cited didn't exist.

This is the part of AI nobody warns you about until it embarrasses you. The tool doesn't sound unsure when it's wrong. It sounds exactly as confident as when it's right. That's the whole problem.

## Why it makes things up

ChatGPT isn't looking anything up. It predicts the next most plausible words based on patterns it learned from text. When it knows the answer, the most plausible words happen to be true. When it doesn't, the most plausible-sounding words are still right there waiting: a real-looking name, a clean-looking number, a citation in the correct format. It writes those with the same fluency. There's no internal moment where it stops and checks whether the thing it just said is real.

In September 2025, OpenAI's own researchers put a finer point on why. Models are trained and graded like students taking a multiple-choice exam: a confident guess might score points, but "I don't know" scores zero. So guessing gets rewarded and admitting uncertainty gets punished — across the millions of examples that shape the model. You end up with a system that would rather bluff than leave the answer blank.

It's worth saying plainly: this isn't a glitch that a future update quietly fixes. Confident guessing is baked into how these tools work.

## Where it bites hardest

The made-up stuff clusters in predictable places. Be on guard whenever you ask for:

- **Specific numbers** — market sizes, percentages, prices, dates.
- **Names and citations** — studies, authors, book titles, case law, URLs.
- **Direct quotes** — what someone "said," word for word.
- **Anything niche** — the more obscure your question, the thinner the real patterns, and the more it fills the gap with fiction.

Two New York lawyers learned this the expensive way. In 2023 they filed a brief full of cases ChatGPT had invented — complete with fake quotes and citation numbers. The opposing side couldn't find the cases. Neither could the judge. The lawyers were sanctioned and fined $5,000. The cases looked perfect. They just weren't real.

## How to catch it before it costs you

You don't need to stop using it. You need to treat its specifics as claims, not facts:

- **Verify anything you'd be embarrassed to be wrong about.** A number going in a deck, a name going in an email, a fact going to a client — check it at the source before it leaves your hands.
- **Ask for the source, then actually open it.** A citation existing in the answer doesn't mean it exists in the world. Click through.
- **Tell it to flag uncertainty.** Add "if you're not sure, say so" to your prompt. It won't be perfect, but it nudges the bluffing down.
- **Scale your checking to the stakes.** Brainstorming a subject line? Don't bother. Quoting a figure to an investor? Verify every digit.

The mental model that saves you: ChatGPT is a fast, confident intern who never says "I'm not sure." Brilliant for drafts and first passes. Never the last word on a fact.

The tool isn't broken. It's doing exactly what it was built to do — sound right. Catching the difference between sounding right and being right is your job, and it takes about thirty seconds. Spend them before the answer reaches anyone who matters.
