You pasted the client contract in to get a summary. The cap table, to model a scenario. The investor email, to sharpen the tone. It worked, it was fast, and nobody told you where any of it went. For most founders, agency owners, and freelancers, that's the whole privacy policy: paste first, never ask.

Here's the part worth a few minutes of your attention, because the answer isn't "you're fine" and it isn't "never touch it."

## Where your pasted data actually goes

On the consumer plans most people use — ChatGPT Free, Plus, and Pro — your conversations are used to train OpenAI's models by default. That setting is on unless you turn it off. So the contract you pasted doesn't just produce a summary and vanish. It can be retained and fed into how the model learns.

On business plans — ChatGPT Business, Enterprise, Edu — that's reversed. Your data is excluded from training by default. Same chatbot, completely different deal with your information, decided by which plan you happen to be on.

Most people pasting sensitive work into ChatGPT are on the personal plan and don't know there's a difference.

## This isn't a rare mistake

It's the norm. Cyberhaven, watching 1.6 million knowledge workers, found that 11% of everything employees pasted into ChatGPT was confidential — and 4.2% of workers had pasted company data at least once. Source code, client records, internal numbers. Not from carelessness. From people moving fast and treating a chat box like a private notepad.

The problem is it isn't private, and it doesn't warn you. The box looks the same whether you paste a blog draft or a signed NDA.

## What you should never paste into the consumer version

Treat these as off-limits unless you're on a business plan that excludes your data from training:

- **Client data you don't own.** Contracts, contact lists, anything covered by an NDA or a confidentiality clause. Leaking your client's information is a faster way to lose them than missing a deadline.
- **Deal and cap-table specifics.** Terms, valuations, LP details, names tied to numbers. The stuff that's damaging precisely because it's real.
- **Regulated or personal data.** Anything that falls under privacy law. In the EU, putting personal data into a tool without a legal basis can run to fines of €20 million or 4% of global turnover.
- **Secrets and credentials.** Passwords, API keys, financial account numbers. Obvious, and pasted constantly anyway.

The test is simple: if a competitor or your client seeing this would cost you the deal or the relationship, it doesn't go in the consumer box.

## Does opting out fix it?

Partly. You can switch training off in Settings, under Data Controls — the toggle is "Improve the model for everyone." Do it. But know what it buys you: it stops new conversations from being used for training going forward. It doesn't delete what you've already pasted, and it doesn't pull anything back out of models already trained. There's also Temporary Chat for one-off sensitive questions, which keeps them out of your history.

Opting out lowers the risk. It doesn't make a consumer account a vault.

## The rule that keeps you fast and safe

You don't have to choose between using AI and protecting your business. You have to match the tool to the data.

- Brainstorming, drafting, public-facing copy, generic questions — consumer ChatGPT is fine. Paste freely.
- Anything client-confidential, deal-sensitive, or regulated — a business-tier plan that excludes your data from training, or a tool built to keep your workspace private.

Sort your work into those two buckets once, and the daily decision disappears. The danger was never using AI on sensitive work. It's pasting it into the wrong door without knowing there were two.
