You pasted the client contract in to get a summary. The cap table, to model a scenario. The investor email, to sharpen the tone. It worked, it was fast, and nobody told you where any of it went. For most founders, agency owners, and freelancers, that's the whole privacy policy: paste first, never ask. The answer isn't "you're fine," and it isn't "never touch it."

## Where your pasted data actually goes

On the consumer plans most people use (ChatGPT Free, Plus, Pro), your conversations train OpenAI's models by default, so the contract you pasted can be retained and fed into how the model learns. On the business plans (Business, Enterprise, Edu) it's reversed: your data is excluded by default. Same chatbot, different deal with your information, and most people pasting sensitive work are on the personal plan without knowing the difference exists.

## This isn't a rare mistake

Cyberhaven, watching 1.6 million knowledge workers, found that 11% of everything employees pasted into ChatGPT was confidential, and 4.2% of workers had pasted company data at least once. Source code, client records, internal numbers, from people moving fast and treating a chat box like a private notepad. It isn't private, and it never warns you. The box looks identical whether you paste a blog draft or a signed NDA.

## What you should never paste into the consumer version

Treat these as off-limits unless you're on a business plan that excludes your data from training:

- **Client data you don't own.** Contracts, contact lists, anything under an NDA. Leaking your client's information loses them faster than missing a deadline.
- **Deal and cap-table specifics.** Terms, valuations, LP details, names tied to numbers. Damaging precisely because it's real.
- **Regulated or personal data.** Anything that falls under privacy law. In the EU, putting personal data into a tool without a legal basis can run to fines of €20 million or 4% of global turnover.
- **Secrets and credentials.** Passwords, API keys, account numbers. Obvious, and pasted constantly anyway.

The test: if a competitor or your client seeing this would cost you the deal or the relationship, it doesn't go in the consumer box.

## Does opting out fix it?

Partly. Switch training off in Settings under Data Controls (the toggle is "Improve the model for everyone"), and use Temporary Chat for one-off sensitive questions. But it only stops new conversations from training the model going forward. It doesn't delete what you've already pasted or pull anything back out of models already trained. Opting out lowers the risk; it doesn't make a consumer account a vault.

## The rule that keeps you fast and safe

You don't have to choose between using AI and protecting your business. Match the tool to the data:

- Brainstorming, drafting, public-facing copy, generic questions: consumer ChatGPT is fine. Paste freely.
- Anything client-confidential, deal-sensitive, or regulated: a business-tier plan that excludes your data from training, or a tool built to keep your workspace private.

Sort your work into those two buckets once, and the daily decision disappears. Most people don't get burned for using AI on sensitive work. They get burned for using the consumer box when a private door was sitting right next to it.
