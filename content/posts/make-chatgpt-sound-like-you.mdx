You wrote the post in ChatGPT, read it back, and something felt off. It was clean, it was correct, and it sounded like every other post in the feed that week. For a founder, agency owner, or freelancer, that's a real problem: your voice is part of what people buy, and when your words sound like a stranger borrowed your account, the trust you've built quietly leaks out. The fix takes about ten minutes, once.

## Why AI writing all sounds the same

A language model writes by predicting the most likely next word, over and over, and "most likely" means the average of everything it has read. Your voice lives at the edges: the odd phrase you reuse, the sentence you'd never finish the expected way, the joke only you'd make. Averaging sands all of that off, leaving something fluent, safe, and anonymous.

Tuning makes it worse. These models are trained to be agreeable and broadly helpful, which collapses them toward one polite tone that belongs to no one. That beige isn't a bug you're triggering. It's the default setting of a machine built to sound like everybody at once.

## Why "write in my voice" doesn't work

"Write this in my voice" does almost nothing, because the model has never read you. It has no idea whether your voice is short and blunt or long and warm, whether you swear, whether you open with a story or a number. Asked for a voice it can't see, it falls back on average "good writing," which is exactly what you were trying to escape. You can't name your voice into existence. You have to show it.

## What actually makes it sound like you

Give it real examples and let it reverse-engineer the pattern:

- Collect three to five pieces you actually wrote and like. Emails, posts, a page from your site. Real ones, not polished-for-the-occasion ones.
- Paste them in and ask it to analyze your style: sentence length, vocabulary, rhythm, how you open and close, what you avoid.
- Have it turn that into a short style guide you can save and reuse, so you're not re-explaining yourself every time.

Then keep feeding it examples on the actual task. "Write this the way I wrote these three" beats any adjective you could pick, because models copy a sample far better than they follow a description.

## The part no prompt can fix

Voice isn't only word choice. It's judgment: the point you're willing to make, the example you reach for, the line you'd cut. The model can match your rhythm and still produce something hollow, because it doesn't know what you actually think.

So make the human calls first. Decide the point, the proof, and the take before you ask for a draft, then let it handle the assembly. You supply the judgment, it supplies the speed.

## The ten-minute setup that sticks

Do it once: build the style guide from your samples, save it in ChatGPT's custom instructions, then hand it real examples plus your decisions on each task. Before you publish, read it out loud and cut the tells, the stock phrases and tidy three-part lists that scream machine.

The goal isn't to fool anyone that a human typed it. It's to make sure you still sound like you.
