Ask a room of founders what AI is and you get two answers. Half treat it like magic: point it at a problem, trust whatever comes back. The other half treat it like a threat they're supposed to have a take on at dinner. Both camps are guessing, and guessing gets expensive the moment you're deciding whether to pay for this stuff, build on it, or hand it real work.

You don't need the math. You need a model of it that holds up, one that tells you what to trust it with and what to check twice. Here's the one I'd give a smart friend over coffee.

## A well-read assistant who never says "I don't know"

Picture someone who has read almost everything ever written and remembers the shape of all of it. Ask them anything and the answer comes back instantly, fluent, in your tone. They also can't bring themselves to admit when they're unsure. They'll invent a statistic with the same confidence they'd quote a real one.

That's a language model. It isn't looking anything up. It's predicting what a good answer sounds like, one word at a time, from the patterns in everything it read. Most of the time the most likely-sounding answer is also the right one, which is why it feels like magic. Your trouble starts the moment those two come apart.

## It isn't thinking, and that's the part to get

The model has no idea whether what it just told you is true. It holds no picture of you, your company, or what's riding on the answer. It isn't reasoning toward a conclusion the way you would; it's matching patterns and producing the next plausible word.

So "it sounded really sure" is worth nothing. Confidence is the one thing it hands you for free in every answer, right or completely invented. Stop reading fluency as competence and you're already ahead of most people using it.

## What it's genuinely good at

There's a clean line through everything it does well: the work where a fast, strong first draft beats a slow perfect one, and where you were going to read the output anyway.

- Turning a blank page into a draft you can react to
- Squeezing a pile of documents, threads, or notes down to the gist
- Rewriting the same thing for a different reader or a different tone
- First-pass research across more text than you'd ever get through yourself

That's a startling share of knowledge work. It lands here for the same reason it fails elsewhere: it's fast, it's fluent, and you're still there to catch the misses.

## Where it'll quietly burn you

The failures are predictable, which is the good news. You can plan around predictable.

- Anything recent, or anything private to your business, sits outside what it read. It won't tell you it's missing the facts. It fills the gap with something that reads right.
- Work where 90% correct is worse than not doing it: figures in a deck, names, dates, the legal and financial specifics. A wrong number delivered with total confidence is a landmine, not a draft.
- Judgment that leans on context it doesn't have. It doesn't know your customers, your history, or what you're actually trying to win.

One rule covers all three. How much a confident wrong answer would cost you is exactly how hard you should check the work.

## How to actually get leverage out of it

Most people use AI as a chatbot they babysit one reply at a time. That's the slow lane. The leverage shows up when you change how you ask.

Give it the context. It can't read your mind, only what you put in front of it, and the gap between a useless answer and a great one is almost always the brief, not the model. Ask for the outcome instead of the steps: "write the investor update from these numbers and last month's," not "help me think about updates." Then check what's expensive to get wrong and let the rest go. Spot-check the figures and the names. Don't proofread its grammar, which is the one thing it never fumbles.

## What to hand off first

Start with the work you'd happily give a sharp assistant and glance at two minutes later: the recurring research-and-draft jobs that eat your mornings. The Monday briefing. The background on a company before a call. The follow-up that's been sitting in your drafts for three days.

Begin where a confident wrong answer costs you a glance, not a client. Get a feel for what it nails and what it fudges, then push it at bigger things. You don't need to know how the engine works to drive. You just need to know it isn't a horse.
