You generated a logo in an AI tool, liked it, and put it on everything: the site, the deck, the invoices. Months later a competitor runs the same prompts, gets something nearly identical, and starts using it too. You call a lawyer to shut them down. The lawyer asks one question that stops you cold: do you actually own it?

For most AI-generated work, the answer is no, not the way you think. And the gap between "I made this" and "I own this" is where founders get burned.

## You can use it. You just can't own it.

These are two separate questions, and people collapse them into one.

Can you use AI output commercially? Almost always yes. The terms of service for the major tools hand you the output to do what you want with it. OpenAI's terms, for example, assign you all rights to what ChatGPT generates and let you sell it, publish it, or put it in paid ads, on the free plan and paid plans alike.

Do you own it the way you own something you wrote or designed yourself? No. In the US, copyright requires a human author. Purely AI-generated work has no human author, so it gets no copyright. The Copyright Office said this plainly in its January 2025 report: typing prompts, even long and clever ones, doesn't make the result yours to copyright, because you're giving instructions and the machine is making the creative calls. Courts have backed this up, and in March 2026 the Supreme Court declined to revisit it.

## What "no copyright" costs you in practice

Copyright is the right to stop other people from copying your thing. No copyright means no right to stop them.

So when a competitor reproduces your AI-generated logo, ad, or jingle, you have little standing to make them quit. The asset you built a brand around is, legally, closer to public property than private. That's fine for a throwaway social graphic. It's a real problem for anything you need to be uniquely yours.

## The bill that can land on your desk

There's a second risk that runs the other direction. AI tools are trained on enormous amounts of existing work, and they sometimes produce output that resembles something already protected, an image close to a real artist's style, copy that echoes a real source.

If that happens and the rights holder comes after you, read your tool's terms before you assume you're covered. Most of them push the liability onto you: you indemnify the company, meaning you pay the legal bills if the output you published turns out to infringe. You took the convenient shortcut, so you carry the risk.

## How to protect what actually matters

The fix isn't to stop using AI. It's to know which outputs need protection and treat those differently.

- **Treat AI as a first draft, not a final asset.** A logo, tagline, or signature visual you'll build a brand on should get real human reworking before it's final. Meaningful creative edits are what can earn copyright on the parts you contributed.
- **Lean on trademark for brand marks.** Copyright isn't the only protection. A logo or business name you actually use in commerce can be defended as a trademark regardless of how it was made. If a mark matters, register it.
- **Read the terms on anything you'll publish widely.** Know what the tool grants you and where it leaves you exposed before the work goes out, not after a letter arrives.
- **Save the human-only work for what's high-stakes.** For a client's flagship deliverable or your core brand, the few hours of real human input buy you protection a prompt never will.

Use AI for the speed. Just don't confuse the receipt it gives you for ownership. The two are not the same thing, and the difference shows up at the worst possible moment.
