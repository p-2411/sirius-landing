Halfway through your investor call, a little bot slides into the participant list: "Sarah's Notetaker." You didn't invite it. Sarah's account did, automatically, and it's been transcribing since the first hello, the soft numbers you floated, the competitor you spoke about. You have no idea where any of it lands. For anyone who closes deals on calls, that's the new default, and almost nobody asked for it.

## Where the recording actually goes

The transcript doesn't live on your laptop. It sits on the vendor's servers, tied to whoever's account ran the bot, and on most consumer tools your meeting feeds the company's models by default. That's the same trap as pasting a contract into ChatGPT, except now it's every word everyone said, captured automatically, with no box to think twice over.

Then it stays. These tools make months of meetings searchable, so an offhand line from a brainstorm can resurface in an audit or a dispute a year later. And the notes belong to the account that recorded, not to you, so a client's casual remark about their budget can sit in a stranger's searchable history with nothing tying your hands to it.

## When it's actually legal to record

This is the part founders skip. Twelve states require every person on the call to agree before you record anything: California, Connecticut, Delaware, Florida, Illinois, Maryland, Massachusetts, Montana, New Hampshire, Oregon, Pennsylvania, and Washington. One participant dialing in from California can pull the whole call under that rule, and it covers in-person rooms too, not just phone and video.

The risk isn't theoretical. In August 2025, a federal class action, Brewer v. Otter.ai, accused the popular notetaker of recording private conversations without everyone's consent, under wiretapping and California privacy law. The core complaint: the bot joins and records when one person's account is connected, while the other people on the call never agreed to anything. If your tool drops into meetings on autopilot, that's the exact behavior being litigated, and you're the one in the room when it happens.

## The etiquette that keeps the client

Legal exposure is the floor. The relationship is the real stakes, and the fix costs about fifteen seconds:

- **Say it out loud before you start.** "I've got a notetaker running for my own notes, all good?" Asking turns a silent recording into a courtesy.
- **Kill it for the sensitive stretch.** When talk turns to numbers, a personnel issue, or anything off the record, pause the bot. Not everything needs a transcript.
- **Offer the notes back.** Sending the client the recap flips the recording from something done to them into something done for them.
- **Default it off for outside calls.** Let it run on internal syncs; switch it on deliberately when a client or investor is in the room.

## One rule that covers most of it

You don't have to choose between good notes and a client's trust. Sort your calls into two buckets. On internal syncs, let the notetaker run and grab everything. The moment someone outside your company is on the line, the recording becomes a decision instead of a default: tell them it's on, pause it when the conversation gets sensitive, and remember that one person dialing in from a state like California can mean everyone has to agree.

Nobody gets burned for taking good notes. They get burned when a bot they forgot they switched on records a room that never agreed to be recorded.
