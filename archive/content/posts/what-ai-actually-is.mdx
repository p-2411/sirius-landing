Most founders treat AI as either magic or a threat. Both are guesses, and guessing gets expensive once you're paying for it or handing it real work. You don't need the math. You need a model that tells you what to trust it with and what to check. Here's mine.

## A well-read assistant who never says "I don't know"

Picture someone who's read almost everything ever written and answers anything instantly, fluent and in your tone, but can't admit when they're unsure. They'll invent a statistic as confidently as they'd quote a real one.

That's a language model. It isn't looking things up; it's predicting what a good answer sounds like, word by word. Usually the likely-sounding answer is also the right one, which is why it feels like magic. Your trouble starts when those two come apart.

## It isn't thinking

The model doesn't know if what it told you is true, and it has no picture of you or your company. Confidence is free in every answer, right or invented, so "it sounded sure" tells you nothing. Stop reading fluency as competence and you're ahead of most people using it.

## What it's good at

Anything where a fast first draft beats a slow perfect one and you'll review the output anyway:

- A blank page turned into a draft you can react to
- A pile of docs or threads boiled down to the gist
- The same message rewritten for a different reader
- First-pass research across more text than you'd read yourself

That's most knowledge work. Use it here freely.

## Where it'll burn you

Check hard, or don't delegate at all, when:

- The answer depends on something recent or private to you. It won't flag the gap; it fills it with something that reads right.
- Being 90% right is worse than not doing it: figures in a deck, names, dates, legal or financial specifics. A confident wrong number is a landmine.
- The call needs context it doesn't have: your customers, your history, what you're trying to win.

The rule: how much a confident wrong answer would cost you is how hard you check.

## How to get leverage

- **Give it the context.** The gap between a useless answer and a great one is the brief, not the model.
- **Ask for the outcome, not the steps.** "Write the investor update from these numbers" beats "help me think about updates."
- **Verify what's expensive, skip the rest.** Spot-check figures and names. Don't proofread its grammar.
- **Hand off whole tasks, not single replies.** Babysitting one prompt at a time is the slow lane.

## What to hand off first

The recurring research-and-draft work that eats your mornings: the Monday briefing, the background on a company before a call, the follow-up sitting in your drafts. Start where a wrong answer costs you a glance, not a client. Learn what it nails and what it fudges, then push it at bigger things.

You don't need to know how the engine works to drive. You just need to know it isn't a horse.
