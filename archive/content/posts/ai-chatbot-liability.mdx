You added an AI chat widget to your site so it could answer questions and book calls while you sleep. Then one day it tells a customer something that isn't true. Who pays for that — you, or "the AI"?

A court has already answered this, and the answer should change how you set the thing up.

## A tribunal already ruled on it

In *Moffatt v. Air Canada*, a man booking a last-minute flight for his grandmother's funeral asked Air Canada's website chatbot about bereavement fares. The bot told him he could book now and claim the discount retroactively. That was false, and it cost him the difference when he tried to claim.

Air Canada's defence was that the chatbot was "a separate legal entity responsible for its own actions." The tribunal rejected that outright: the bot is part of your website, so its words are your words. Air Canada was ordered to pay. The money was small, about $812. The principle is the expensive part. You own what your bot says.

## Why "the AI said it, not us" doesn't hold up

The only reason a customer trusts that answer is that it appeared on your site, under your brand. A tribunal treats it the same as any other statement on your page, or any staff member giving advice at a desk. If a customer reasonably relies on it and loses money, that is negligent misrepresentation, regardless of whether a human or a model typed it.

The automation doesn't dilute your responsibility. You chose to put an unsupervised voice on your website and point customers at it.

## The catch: it won't bind you to something absurd

There is a limit. A Chevrolet dealership's ChatGPT-powered bot once got talked into "selling" a Tahoe for a dollar, complete with "that's a legally binding offer, no takesies backsies." That one wasn't binding. The bot had no authority to price or sell cars, and the buyer had deliberately instructed it to agree to anything.

So the line runs like this: you are on the hook for what a customer would reasonably believe and act on, meaning your real prices, policies, eligibility rules and promises. You are not on the hook for a stunt someone engineered to go viral. But don't lean on that defence. Air Canada's bot wasn't provoked. It simply invented a plausible-sounding policy, and plausible-but-wrong is exactly where the risk lives.

## How to add one without inheriting its mistakes

- **Don't let it invent policy.** Give it your actual refund, pricing, warranty and eligibility rules, or have it link to the page rather than paraphrase from memory.
- **Keep its authority narrow.** It can answer and route. It cannot approve refunds, quote custom prices, or make guarantees. Anything touching money or commitments goes to a person.
- **Make it cite the source.** "Here's our bereavement policy" with a link beats a confident paragraph the bot wrote on its own.
- **Test it like an adversary before launch.** Ask the questions that cost you money: refunds, cancellations, discounts, deadlines. See what it actually says.
- **Read the transcripts.** If you never look at what your bot is telling customers, you'll find out the way Air Canada did.

## The takeaway

An AI chatbot on your site isn't a robot shielded by a disclaimer. Treat it as staff. Whatever it tells a customer, you told them. Set it up so the only things it can say are things you would stand behind, and it will do the job you hired it for without writing cheques you never agreed to.
