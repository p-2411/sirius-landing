You added the chatbot to handle the repetitive questions, and it does. It answers at 2am, it never gets tired, it saves your team hours. Then one day it tells a customer something you never approved: a refund window, a discount, a delivery date that isn't real. The customer takes a screenshot and holds you to it. The question that decides how much this costs you is simple: is your business on the hook for what the bot said? Increasingly, the answer is yes.

## The case that settled the question

A man named Jake Moffatt asked Air Canada's website chatbot about bereavement fares after his grandmother died. The bot told him he could buy a full-price ticket and claim the discount back within 90 days. No such policy existed. When Air Canada refused the refund, he took it to a tribunal, and Air Canada's defense was that the chatbot was "a separate legal entity" responsible for its own answers.

The tribunal rejected that outright. In its words, the chatbot "is still just a part of Air Canada's website," and "it makes no difference whether the information comes from a static page or a chatbot." Air Canada was found liable for negligent misrepresentation and ordered to pay up. The money was small, around CA$812. The precedent is the expensive part: your bot speaks for your business the same way an employee does, and "the AI made it up" is not a defense.

## It doesn't take a lawsuit to hurt

In April 2025, the AI support bot for the coding tool Cursor told users that their subscription was now limited to one device, framed as a security feature. There was no such policy. The bot invented it. Customers who worked across a laptop and a desktop read the fake rule and started canceling, and the screenshots spread across Reddit and Hacker News before anyone at the company noticed. The co-founder had to publicly apologize and confirm the policy never existed.

No court, no damages award, just churn and a bruised reputation. A chatbot doesn't need to break a law to cost you. It only needs to sound confident about something untrue, to exactly the people you're trying to keep.

## Why it invents things in the first place

This catches people off guard because they assume the bot is reading their policy page and quoting it. It isn't. It predicts a plausible-sounding answer from patterns in its training, and your actual policy is only one of the things shaping that guess. When a customer asks about an edge case your help docs don't cover, a refund exception, a warranty detail, the bot doesn't say "I don't know." It fills the gap with an answer that reads like it belongs on your site. To the customer, that answer is your company talking, in writing, on the record.

## How to fence yours in

- **Limit what it's allowed to discuss.** Let it handle hours, order status, and how-to questions. Route anything that costs money, refunds, pricing, contract terms, guarantees, to a human.
- **Say it's a bot, and say what it can't do.** A clear line that the assistant can't approve refunds or make binding commitments reduces the "apparent authority" a court weighs.
- **Keep the transcripts.** If a customer claims the bot promised something, the log is your evidence, in both directions.
- **Try to break it yourself.** Before a customer does, spend twenty minutes trying to talk your bot into offering a discount or a refund it shouldn't. What you can extract, so can they.

The safe way to think about it: your chatbot is a junior hire you can't fully train, and anything it says, you said. Decide what it's allowed to promise, put a person between it and anything that touches money, and check its work the way you'd check a new employee's client emails, before they go out.
