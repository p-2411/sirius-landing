You wrote the copy yourself. You delivered it, the client ran it through some AI detector, and now there's an email in your inbox: "This came back 82% AI-generated." Suddenly you're defending work you actually did, your invoice is in question, and there's no obvious way to prove a machine wrong. If you sell writing, or ship anything with words on it, this is the accusation you can't argue your way out of with a straight face.

So it's worth knowing what these detectors can and can't do.

## Are AI detectors accurate?

Short version: no, not reliably. The most telling proof comes from OpenAI, the company that makes ChatGPT. It built its own tool to spot AI writing, and then quietly killed it in July 2023, citing a "low rate of accuracy." By its own published numbers, that detector correctly caught AI text only 26% of the time, while falsely flagging genuine human writing as AI 9% of the time. The people who built the AI couldn't build a reliable detector for it.

Everyone else's tool is guessing at the same problem. Detectors have confidently labeled the US Constitution as AI-written. A score that can flag a 1787 document has no business deciding whether you wrote last week's landing page.

## Why human writing gets flagged as AI

Detectors don't actually recognize AI. They measure how *predictable* your writing is, because AI models are built to produce the most likely next word. Clean, simple, well-structured prose reads as "predictable" to a detector, so the clearer and more professional you write, the more likely you are to trip one. You get punished for being good at the job.

The bias runs in one specific direction that should worry anyone hiring globally. A 2023 Stanford study found detectors misclassified more than 61% of essays by non-native English speakers as AI-generated, while scoring native writers near-perfectly. If your contractor, VA, or offshore team writes solid second-language English, a detector is close to a coin flip on their work, and the coin is weighted against them.

## Even universities stopped trusting them

Schools have every reason to catch AI, and the ones with the most at stake gave up on detection. Vanderbilt disabled Turnitin's AI detector in 2023. Turnitin advertised a 1% false-positive rate, which sounds tiny until you do the math Vanderbilt did: across the 75,000 papers it processes in a year, that's roughly 750 students wrongly flagged. More than a dozen major universities, including Yale and Northwestern, turned these tools off entirely.

The lesson for your business is simple. If institutions with legal departments won't treat a detector score as proof, neither should you or your client.

## What to do when your work gets flagged

The score is not evidence, so don't argue the score. Argue the process, and keep proof of it.

- **Write in a tool that saves history.** Google Docs and Word both keep version history that shows your draft forming over hours. That trail is far harder to fake than any detector is to pass, and it's the closest thing to an alibi you have.
- **Set the terms before you start.** Add one line to your agreement: AI-detection scores are known to produce false positives and won't be used as grounds to withhold payment. Have that conversation before the work, not during the dispute.
- **Reframe, calmly.** Send the client one link to OpenAI retiring its own detector, and offer your version history. You're not defending yourself; you're informing them their tool is unreliable.
- **If you do use AI, make the work yours.** Edit it enough that it carries your judgment and your voice, and keep your drafting visible. Then the honest answer to "did you use AI" is one you can stand behind.

A detector score is a guess wearing the costume of a verdict. Your saved drafts are the actual record. Keep them, and the accusation stops being something that happens *to* you.
