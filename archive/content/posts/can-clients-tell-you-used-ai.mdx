You sent over the finished piece — the launch copy, the report, the proposal — and you were proud of it. Hours later the reply lands: "Hey, I ran this through an AI detector and it came back 88% AI. Can we talk?" Now you're defending work you did, the score gets treated like a lie detector, and you're the one who has to explain yourself. So it's worth knowing exactly what that tool measured, because it wasn't what your client thinks.

## Do AI detectors actually work?

Not reliably enough to accuse anyone. The clearest proof came from the company best placed to build one. In July 2023, OpenAI quietly shut down its own AI-text detector, citing a "low rate of accuracy." In its own tests, the tool correctly flagged just 26% of AI-written text, while wrongly tagging nearly one in ten pieces of genuine human writing as AI. The maker of ChatGPT couldn't reliably catch ChatGPT, so it stopped pretending it could. The detectors your client is using are not better than the one OpenAI gave up on.

## What a high "AI score" is really measuring

These tools don't recognize AI. They measure how predictable your writing is — how closely each word follows the path a model would most likely take. Plain, clean, well-organized prose scores as "AI" because that's what low-surprise writing looks like, no matter who typed it. The score is a guess about style, dressed up as a verdict about authorship.

Which is how you get absurd results. Feed these tools the US Constitution and they'll tell you the Founding Fathers used ChatGPT; one detector rated the Declaration of Independence roughly 98% AI-written. The bias isn't random, either. A 2023 Stanford study found seven popular detectors falsely flagged about 61% of essays by non-native English speakers as AI, while clearing native writers almost perfectly. Write in tidy, straightforward English and you look *more* like a machine to these tools, not less.

## What to do when a client runs one

- **Don't panic or confess.** A detector score isn't evidence. It's a probability guess from a tool whose own field can't validate it.
- **Say what's true.** These tools are documented as unreliable and routinely flag human writing. The Constitution example does a lot of work in one sentence.
- **Show your process.** Drafts, notes, the brief, version history. A paper trail beats a score every time.

And if you're the one tempted to run a contractor's work through a detector before paying: don't hang a decision on it. Sooner or later you'll accuse someone honest.

## The move that actually protects you

The instinct is to grab a "humanizer" to beat the detector. Skip it. Researchers at the University of Pennsylvania found detectors are easily fooled by light edits anyway, so it's a race you don't need to run, and it keeps your attention on disguising the work instead of improving it.

The durable answer is to make the work unmistakably yours. AI can hand you the beige first draft. What the client is actually paying for is the part it can't produce: your specific facts, your read on their situation, the example only you would think to use, the opinion you'll put your name behind. Add those and the detector question quietly stops mattering, because the work stops being generic, and stops sounding like anyone with a login could have made it.

You used a tool. So does everyone being honest about it. The thing worth defending was never that you didn't. It's that the result is good, true, and clearly yours.
