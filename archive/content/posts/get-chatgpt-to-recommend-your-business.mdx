A founder you've never met just asked ChatGPT to recommend an agency like yours. It gave them three names. You weren't one of them. There was no ranking page to check, no ad you lost, no click that went somewhere else, the question simply got answered and you weren't in the answer. This is how a growing share of your buyers shop now, and most businesses have no idea whether they're even in the room.

## Your buyers stopped clicking

The search box quietly became an answer box. When Google shows an AI summary at the top, people read it and move on instead of clicking through to anyone's site. A randomized field experiment by researchers at the Indian School of Business and Carnegie Mellon, published in 2026, found that those AI Overviews cut clicks to outside websites by about 38% on the searches where they appear, and pushed roughly seven in ten of those searches to end with no click at all. Their satisfaction didn't drop, so they're not coming back to dig deeper.

Meanwhile ChatGPT answers questions for around 800 million people a week. The visibility you used to earn by ranking is increasingly read aloud by a machine that may never say your name.

## Why your homepage isn't enough

When someone asks ChatGPT "who's the best X for Y," it isn't reading your website and deciding you're great. It builds the answer from what the rest of the internet says about businesses like yours: reviews, roundups, comparison articles, forum threads, directories. Your own site is one voice in that pile, and the one it trusts least, because you're the only party guaranteed to be biased in your favor. If the only place that calls you the best is your own About page, you won't get named.

## What gets you into the answer

- **Get into the lists.** The "best [your category] for [your niche]" roundups and comparison posts are exactly what these models quote. One placement in a well-ranked list can surface you across ChatGPT, Gemini, and Google's AI summaries at once. Find the writers who run them and pitch.
- **Collect real reviews, in public.** Models lean on consensus. A steady stream of specific, recent reviews on the platforms your buyers already trust moves you more than another homepage redesign.
- **Answer the question people actually type.** Write the page that matches the real query, "best bookkeeper for SaaS startups," not "financial clarity for modern teams." Plain, specific, fact-dense pages get quoted. Clever ones get skipped.
- **Say the same thing everywhere.** Same name, same one-line description of what you do and who you do it for, across your site, your profiles, and every directory you're listed in. Mixed signals make you easy to leave out.

## How to know if it's working

Open ChatGPT and ask it what your buyer would ask: "Recommend a [what you do] for [who you serve]." Do it a few times, because the answers vary between runs. Note whether you appear, and who shows up when you don't. That list is your real competitive set now, and the distance between you and the names on it is the work. Check again next month.

None of this is a new growth hack. It's the old answer to a new question: be the business other people vouch for, in the places a machine can read. The companies that win the next few years won't have the cleverest website. They'll be the ones the AI already trusts enough to name.
