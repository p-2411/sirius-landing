You wrote the piece yourself. Every sentence, no shortcuts. The client ran it through a detector, and it came back "82% AI." Now they're questioning the invoice, and you're in the strange position of trying to prove you did your own work. For freelancers and agencies, this is a new and genuinely infuriating way to lose a client, and it has almost nothing to do with whether you actually used AI.

## Why clean writing gets flagged

Detectors don't see your process. They can't tell that you spent three hours on a draft. They look at the finished text and guess, based on how predictable and even the wording is. AI tends to produce smooth, tidy, low-surprise sentences, so that's the fingerprint they hunt for.

Here's the problem: that fingerprint is exactly what good professional writing looks like. Clean grammar, a logical flow, plain vocabulary, no weird detours. You spent years learning to write that way. A detector reads it and shrugs that it looks machine-made, because to a pattern-matcher, polish and AI are the same thing.

## The tools can't actually tell

This isn't a case of picking a better detector. The whole category is shaky, and the companies building these tools know it.

OpenAI, the maker of ChatGPT, built its own AI-text detector and quietly killed it six months later, in July 2023, citing a "low rate of accuracy." Their own numbers were bleak: it caught only 26% of AI-written text while falsely flagging 9% of human writing as machine-made. The company best positioned to detect its own output gave up.

Independent research is worse. A 2023 Stanford study ran essays by non-native English speakers through seven popular detectors. On average, 61% of those real, human-written essays were flagged as AI. One detector flagged 97% of them. The essays were written by actual people, and the tools called them fake more often than not.

## Who gets falsely accused most

The false positives aren't random. They cluster on specific kinds of writers:

- **Non-native English speakers**, whose sentences tend to be simpler and more standard, the exact pattern detectors read as artificial.
- **Heavily edited work**, because revision smooths out the little bumps and irregularities that detectors count as human.
- **Anyone who writes plainly and cleanly**, which is to say, most professionals doing their job well.

The irony is sharp. The better and clearer your writing, the more it can look like a machine wrote it.

## What to do when you're accused

Don't try to "beat the detector" by roughing up your prose or pasting it through a humanizer tool. You'll make your own work worse to satisfy a broken test, and you'll still get flagged next time.

Do this instead:

- **Keep the receipts.** Write in Google Docs or Word with version history on. The edit trail, showing hours of typing, deleting, and rewriting, is real proof of a human process. A detector's guess is not.
- **Set the terms before you start.** In your contract or kickoff note, say plainly that AI-detection scores are unreliable and won't be used as grounds to reject or withhold payment. Point to OpenAI shutting down its own tool if you need backup.
- **Reframe the conversation.** A detector produces a probability, not a verdict. When a client treats "82% AI" as proof, your job is to explain, calmly, that the number is a guess from a tool its own makers couldn't make work.

A detector score is an accusation dressed up as a measurement. Treat it that way, keep your version history, and set expectations before the work starts, and a bad number becomes a conversation instead of a lost client.
