Every tool you already pay for has quietly relabeled its chatbot an "AI agent." Your CRM has one, your inbox has one, the calendar app has one. You can't tell what changed, or whether any of it does something the chatbot didn't. There is a real difference underneath the noise, and once you see it, the sales pages get easy to read.

## The difference in one line

A chatbot answers when you ask. An agent does the work when you're not there.

A chatbot is a fast intern who only speaks when spoken to: you type, it replies, and nothing happens until you type again. An agent is one you hand a standing job. It has a starting gun (a time, a new email, a filled-out form), it takes several steps on its own, it uses your actual tools, and it hands back something finished. You set it up once and it runs without you in the room.

That's the whole test. If the thing only talks back, it's a chatbot wearing a new word.

## Why everything suddenly calls itself an agent

Because "agent" sells right now, and slapping the label on old software is cheaper than building the real thing. Gartner has a name for it: agent washing, the rebranding of chatbots, assistants, and old automation tools that don't actually have agent behavior. Out of the thousands of vendors claiming to sell agents, Gartner estimates only around 130 are the real article.

So most of what you're being pitched is the chatbot you already had, repriced. Knowing the one-line test above is what keeps you from paying agent money for chatbot work.

## How to spot a real one before you pay

Run any "agent" through four questions:

- **Does it start on its own?** A real agent has a trigger: a schedule, an incoming message, an event. If you have to open it and type every time, it's a chatbot.
- **Does it take several steps?** Look something up, decide, act, write the result. One question in, one answer out is a chatbot.
- **Does it touch your real tools?** It should read your inbox, your docs, your CRM, not just chat in its own little box.
- **Does it hand back a finished thing?** A drafted email ready to send, a brief, a built report, not a wall of text you now have to assemble yourself.

Four yeses is an agent. Anything less is a chat window with ambition.

## What's actually worth handing to one

Give an agent recurring work that has a fixed shape and a clear finished output. The Monday pipeline summary, the background brief before every sales call, the weekly client report that always pulls from the same places. Define it once, and it's done before you sit down.

What to keep on a short leash: anything where a confident mistake is expensive, and anything that needs judgment only you have. Gartner expects more than 40% of agentic AI projects to be scrapped by the end of 2027, mostly because people aimed them at fuzzy, high-stakes goals the tools can't actually carry yet. Start where being wrong costs you a glance, not a client.

The label on the box tells you nothing. Ask what happens when you walk away from your desk. If the answer is "nothing until I come back and type," you're looking at a chatbot. If the answer is "the work gets done," that's the one worth paying for.
