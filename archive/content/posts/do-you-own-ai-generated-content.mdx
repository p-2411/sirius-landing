You delivered the brand package fast: logo, hero images, launch copy, all built with AI, and the client loved it. A month later a competitor ships something that looks suspiciously close, and your client asks you to make them stop. Here's the part nobody mentions when they sell you the AI tool: you may have nothing to stop them with. Under US law, the work you handed over might belong to no one, which means anyone is free to copy it.

## Owning the file and owning the rights are two different things

A tool's terms of service can hand you the output. Midjourney says you own the assets you create, with a catch: companies past roughly $1M in annual revenue have to be on a paid plan to keep that ownership. OpenAI gives you the images and the right to sell them.

But that's a contract between you and the tool. It is not copyright. Copyright is the thing that lets you stop someone else from using your work, and the tool can't grant it, because it doesn't have it to give.

## Why purely AI-made work has no owner

Copyright in the US protects human authorship, and only human authorship. In March 2025 a federal appeals court ruled in *Thaler v. Perlmutter* that a work generated by an AI on its own can't be registered, because the law's "author" has to be a person. The Supreme Court declined to take the case up in 2026, so that reading is settled for now.

The Copyright Office landed in the same place in its 2025 report on the question. Typing prompts, even long and careful ones, doesn't make you the author, because you don't actually control what the model produces from them. No human author means no copyright. No copyright means the work isn't yours to protect, and it isn't anyone else's either. A competitor copying it isn't infringing, because there's nothing there to infringe.

## The comic that drew the line

A real case shows exactly where the line sits. An artist registered a graphic novel, *Zarya of the Dawn*, with images she had made in Midjourney. The Copyright Office let her keep copyright on the words she wrote and on how she selected and arranged everything, the human parts of the book. It cancelled protection on the AI images themselves, on the grounds that she didn't form them, the model did.

Same book, split decision: your contribution is protected, the machine's raw output isn't.

## How to actually own what you ship

The fix isn't to stop using AI. It's to make sure a person is doing real creative work on top of it:

- **Treat AI output as a draft, not the deliverable.** Edit it, combine pieces, redraw, rewrite. Your changes are yours.
- **Keep the human-made layer.** Original copy, the layout, and the selection and arrangement of elements all carry copyright even when individual generated assets don't.
- **When you register, disclose the AI parts** and claim only your own contribution. That's what the Copyright Office requires, and skipping it can void the whole registration.
- **Don't promise a client exclusivity on untouched AI output.** You can't deliver it, and a rival can legally reuse the same look.

None of this is a reason to stop using AI for client work. You can, and you should. Just get clear on what you're actually handing over. If a person shaped it, you own that work and can defend it. If the machine made it alone, you own a file, not a right, and so does everyone who copies it.
