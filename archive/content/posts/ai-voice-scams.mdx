A finance worker at a global engineering firm joined what looked like a routine video call. His CFO was on it. So were colleagues whose faces and voices he knew. They walked him through an urgent, confidential transfer, and over the course of a day he sent fifteen wires worth about $25.6 million. Every person on that call was a deepfake. The firm, Arup, never got the money back.

You don't have to run a Fortune 500 to be the target. If your voice exists on a podcast, a webinar, an earnings call, or one Instagram video, that's enough to copy it.

## How an AI voice scam actually works

Today's tools can build a convincing version of someone's voice from a few seconds of recorded audio. Nobody has to hack you to get it. They pull it from what you've already published in public: a conference talk, a sales webinar, a YouTube clip, even your voicemail greeting.

Then they stage the pressure. A message that looks like it came from you or a senior partner, a call in "your" voice confirming it, sometimes a video to close the deal. The request is urgent, confidential, and framed as "handle this now, don't loop anyone in." A familiar voice plus a ticking clock plus secrecy is the entire play.

## Why "but it sounded exactly like him" no longer proves anything

For your whole career, recognizing someone's voice was proof enough. That assumption is now the hole in the wall. A media-forensics expert who spent decades teaching people to spot fakes recently told reporters he can't reliably tell cloned audio from the real thing anymore. If he can't hear the seam, your bookkeeper on a Friday afternoon won't either. The voice has stopped being evidence.

## The one habit that stops it cold

Call back. Before anyone moves money or hands over sensitive data, they hang up and reach the person on a number they already have, from the company directory or their own contacts, never the number that just called. The scammer owns the inbound line. They can't answer a callback to the real person's phone. The FTC gives the public the same advice for the consumer version of this scam: stop, and verify on a number you trust.

It feels rude to doubt your own CEO. So make it policy, and nobody has to decide whether to be rude.

## Build verification into how money moves

Make checking the default for anything that touches money, instead of a judgment call someone makes while being rushed:

- **A team code word.** Agree on a word or phrase that isn't findable online and has to be said out loud before any payment goes through. A cloned voice can't produce a word it was never trained on.
- **Two-person approval** on any wire or change to vendor payment details above a set amount. One cornered person is the target; a second, calmer person breaks the spell.
- **Say the red flags out loud** so your team recognizes them in the moment: urgency, secrecy, and a push toward payments you can't claw back, like wires, crypto, or gift cards.

None of this needs new software. It's three rules and one phone call.

So stop treating a familiar voice as proof. Treat the callback as proof. The businesses that lose the money are the ones where one person could approve a transfer alone, in a hurry, because the voice sounded right.
